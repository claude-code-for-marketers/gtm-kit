# Competitor Radar — Newsletter Radar

Last updated: 2026-06-12

> **Note:** Newsletter Radar is pre-launch. Win/loss patterns below are working hypotheses based on
> each competitor's public positioning and the structural gap Newsletter Radar is built to exploit —
> a **brand/advertiser-facing** view of the newsletter sponsorship market, rather than a
> publisher-facing one. Replace hypotheses with real data after the first competitive deals.

---

## The Category

"Newsletter sponsor (ad) intelligence" — tools that track which brands sponsor which newsletters
and expose the relationship as a searchable database. **Every established player is sold primarily
to newsletter publishers** trying to find advertisers to pitch. The same data, read in reverse,
serves the buy side: advertisers deciding where to spend and benchmarking against competitors.

**Newsletter Radar's wedge:** point the data at the advertiser, not the publisher. The two
questions we answer that the incumbents bury:
1. *"Where has this company advertised?"* — pull a brand's entire newsletter footprint.
2. *"Which newsletters should we sponsor?"* — recommend placements from competitor and lookalike spend.

## SponsorSignals  ⚠ closest competitor

> **Verified from sponsorsignals.com (2026-06-15)** — figures and copy below are taken directly from
> their site, not a hypothesis. Founder: Sayz Lim (solo-founder signals: "direct line to the founder").

**One-line summary:** Newsletter sponsorship intelligence that serves **both** sides — "Find brands to
pitch. Find newsletters to sponsor." It has a **fully shipped buy-side product** ("See which newsletters
your competitors sponsor… exact brands, dates, and creative… built for a startup marketer deciding where
to advertise this quarter"), concentrated in **our exact verticals.** Treat this as the benchmark to beat,
not a future threat — they already occupy the wedge.

**Scale (verified):** **170 newsletters**, **2,825 advertisers**, **22,335 verified placements**,
**manually verified daily**. In the last 30 days sponsors ran in 82% of tracked newsletters (140/170).

**Vertical mix (their category counts):** Developer Tools 37 · AI 28 · SaaS & Business 24 · Product &
Startups 22 · Macro & Markets 12 · Crypto & Web3 11 · Marketing & Growth 11 · Startups & VC 11 · Cloud &
Infra 8 · Security 5 · Design 4 · Fintech 4 · Data 3. **This is precisely our launch coverage map.**

**Their strengths (be honest — these are strong):**
- **Buy-side is fully shipped**, not a byproduct. Their pricing page *is* a buy-side pitch.
- Per-advertiser search (enter a brand/URL) → competitor profiles, month-by-month cadence, **exact creative**
- **Status Signals** page: real-time sponsor movement — Scaling / Reactivated / Active / New / Cooling, timestamped to the hour ("50m ago"). A sophisticated freshness layer.
- **MCP access shipped** (Codex, Claude, others) on Pilot/Annual — AI tools research competitors and draft briefs against their data. *This is live, not roadmap.*
- Discovery dashboard with a Not-Seen / Seen / Bookmarks workflow (low cognitive load)
- A clean value ladder: free teaser → $100 one-time → $1k pilot → $5k annual

**Their weaknesses (our real, narrower openings):**
- **Coverage breadth + automation.** 170 newsletters, **manually verified daily** — that doesn't scale.
  Our automated crawl targets **1,000+** (≈6×). *This is our strongest, most defensible edge — if crawl
  quality holds.* Lead here, not on "we're buy-side."
- **No explicit placement plan / gap analysis.** They show the footprint and discovery; we turn it into a
  **ranked buy** — the newsletters your competitors own that you're missing (`radar gaps`). Narrow gap,
  but real. Build and protect it.
- **Split audience** (publishers + advertisers) pulls their roadmap two ways; we're buy-side only.
- Manual verification caps freshness/breadth as the market grows; an automated crawl can outrun it.

**Pricing (verified — their value ladder):**
- **Free:** sponsor search + demo dashboard (no account) + free monthly sponsorship report — a teaser, not full access
- **Validation Pass — $100 / 7 days**, one-time, no auto-renew, no call. Full web access. *"Built for a startup marketer deciding where to advertise this quarter."*
- **Pilot — $1,000 / 3 months**, team, MCP access, credits toward Annual
- **Annual — $5,000 / year, up to 10 seats**, MCP access, priority support, founder access
- ⚠ **Pricing threat:** their **$100 one-time pass** is a far lower-friction buy-side entry than our
  $99/mo (no free tier). A "deciding this quarter" marketer buys the $100 look, not a subscription. See `profile.md`.

**Why an advertiser might still choose us:**
- Broader coverage — "they track 170 newsletters by hand; we crawl 1,000+ automatically"
- The plan, not just the data — "they show the footprint; we hand you the ranked buy and the gaps you're missing"
- Buy-side only — no sell-side clutter in the workflow

**How to handle "isn't this just SponsorSignals?":**
> "SponsorSignals is the closest thing out there and genuinely serves advertisers — credit to them. Two
> differences: coverage and output. They verify ~170 newsletters by hand; we crawl 1,000+ automatically,
> so you're not blind outside their panel. And they stop at the footprint — we turn it into a ranked plan
> of where to spend next, including the placements your competitors own that you don't."

**Reality check (read this).** SponsorSignals largely *is* the product we describe on our site, already
shipped, in our verticals, with MCP and a cheap entry point. "Buy-side" is **not** a differentiator
against them. Our defensible edges narrow to: (1) **coverage breadth via automated crawl**, (2) the
**placement plan/gap analysis**, (3) focus + UX. The pricing stance (no free tier, subscription-only)
needs a rethink against their $100 pass. Monitor weekly; revisit our differentiation and pricing now.

## Who Sponsors Stuff

**One-line summary:** The deepest *historical* archive in the category, with an ad-image library.
The category's incumbent — but built as a prospecting database for publishers' ad-sales teams.

**Market position:** Most established player. Tracks 250+ newsletters and ~4,000 sponsor companies.
Searchable by keyword or filtered by vertical. Paid **Sales Pro** tier adds direct contact info plus
images of the actual ads a company has run elsewhere.

**Their strengths:**
- Deepest historical archive — years of sponsor history per newsletter
- Ad-creative image library (you can see the actual ads a brand ran)
- Established brand and trust in the publisher community

**Their weaknesses (our openings):**
- Framed for the **sell side** — UX and contact data are about pitching advertisers, not helping advertisers decide
- Coverage is a fixed ~250-newsletter panel; misses the long tail
- Archive skews historical — weaker on "who started advertising this week"
- The reverse lookup ("everywhere this brand advertised") exists but is a byproduct, not the headline

**Why an advertiser would switch to us:**
- "I wanted to see every newsletter my competitor is in, ranked by spend — not scroll a sponsor directory built for sellers"
- "Their data felt like an archive; I needed what's running now"

**How to handle "we already check Who Sponsors Stuff":**
> "It's the best historical archive out there — most serious advertisers have looked at it. The gap
> we hear about is that it's built to help newsletters find advertisers, so when you flip it around to
> 'where should *we* spend and where are competitors spending right now,' you're doing the translation
> yourself. That's the part we built for the buy side."

## SponsorGap

**One-line summary:** Newer, real-time competitor. Search by niche, industry, or company name to see
brands actively buying sponsorships, with spend trends and ad frequency. Surfaces newly funded companies.

**Market position:** Real-time challenger to Who Sponsors Stuff. Basic search reportedly works without
signup; contact data sits behind a paid plan.

**Their strengths:**
- Real-time view of who's actively buying (vs. a historical archive)
- Spend-trend and ad-frequency data
- Funding-event surfacing (newly raised companies = likely new ad budget)
- Low-friction entry (search before paying)

**Their weaknesses (our openings):**
- Still publisher-prospecting in framing — the output is a lead list, not an advertiser's media plan
- No (public) recommendation layer: it tells you who's spending, not where *you* should spend
- Brand-profile depth is thinner than Appeared.in

**Why an advertiser would switch to us:**
- "SponsorGap told me which brands are active; I needed it to tell me which newsletters fit *my* audience and budget"

**When to compete vs. concede:** Compete on advertiser workflow (planning, benchmarking, recommendations).
Concede that they're strong on raw real-time spend signal — then reframe to "signal is step one; the plan is the product."

## Appeared.in

**One-line summary:** The most modern-feeling tool. Real-time sponsorship tracking with rich brand
profiles (funding, revenue, size) and per-brand feature history — essentially a company→newsletters view.

**Market position:** Sold to mid-to-large newsletters and agencies managing ad sales. Thousands of
newsletters tracked. Includes verified contacts, ad previews, lookalike sponsor matching, and CRM
integrations (Apollo, HubSpot, Clay, Sheets).

**Their strengths:**
- Cleanest, most modern UX in the category
- Rich brand profiles: funding, revenue, company size
- Per-brand "feature history" — closest thing to our reverse-lookup view
- Lookalike matching and CRM integrations

**Their weaknesses (our openings):**
- Audience is still **publishers and ad-sales agencies**, not the advertiser deciding where to spend
- Lookalike matching is tuned to "find similar *sponsors* to pitch," not "find similar *newsletters* to buy"
- Their per-brand history is buyer-relevant but positioned as a sales-prospecting feature

**Why an advertiser would switch to us:**
- "Appeared has great brand profiles, but the whole product assumes I'm selling sponsorships. I'm buying them."

**This is the closest competitor on data; the wedge is audience and workflow, not data depth.**
Watch them most — if they ship an explicit advertiser/buy-side mode, the differentiation narrows fast.

## Paved Radar

**One-line summary:** AI scans 15,000+ newsletters to detect sponsorships and pipes real-time leads
(newsletter + sponsoring brand + verified contact) into the user's sales tools.

**Market position:** Lead-gen feature inside Paved's broader newsletter-advertising marketplace.
Built for publishers; integrates via Zapier/Make. Widest raw coverage (15,000+ newsletters).

**Their strengths:**
- Largest newsletter coverage in the category (15,000+)
- AI detection at scale, delivered as a real-time lead feed
- Tied into the Paved marketplace and outbound tooling

**Their weaknesses (our openings):**
- Purely a **lead feed for publishers** — no advertiser-side analysis at all
- Gravity toward the Paved marketplace; not a neutral planning tool
- Output is "here's a lead," not "here's where your budget should go"

**Why an advertiser would switch to us:**
- "Paved Radar is for newsletters selling ad slots. There's nothing in it for me as the buyer."

**When to compete:** Rarely head-to-head — different buyer. Cite their 15,000-newsletter coverage as
proof the *market data exists*, then differentiate on who the product is for.

## Win/Loss Patterns  *(pre-launch hypotheses; validate after first deals)*

**Why we expect to win:**
1. The buyer is an **advertiser/brand**, not a publisher — every incumbent makes them translate
2. They want competitor benchmarking ("where is X advertising") as a first-class workflow
3. They want a recommendation/plan, not a sponsor directory or lead list
4. Freshness matters to them and they've felt an archive go stale

**Why we expect to lose:**
1. A prospect only needs raw "who's spending" signal → SponsorGap/Appeared already suffice
2. The newsletter they care about isn't in our coverage panel (finite-coverage caveat)
3. **SponsorSignals already serves advertisers** and offers a free tier + cheap pilot — a price-sensitive prospect tries them first
4. SponsorSignals (or Appeared.in) ships a placement-*plan* layer and matches our core differentiation
5. Prospect is actually a publisher → we're the wrong tool; refer out or expand later

## Strategic Decisions  *(resolved 2026-06-15)*

- **Buy-side only.** We do not serve publishers. Publishers are anti-ICP. Note: this is a *focus* claim,
  not a *uniqueness* claim — **SponsorSignals already serves advertisers too** (see its battlecard). Our
  edge is being buy-side *only* (sharper than their split audience) and shipping the placement **plan**,
  not just the intelligence. Avoid the absolute "we're the only buy-side tool" line — it's not true.
- **Coverage:** our **own crawl**, 1,000+ newsletters at launch, in tech / AI / business / finance (+ adjacent
  like crypto), expanding by vertical. We won't out-cover Paved's 15,000 — we win on buy-side workflow,
  vertical depth, and freshness. Use 1,000+ as proof the data is real and current, not as a size claim.
- **Pure intelligence.** No contact data, no outreach — deliberately *not* a lead list. This is the
  cleanest line of separation from every incumbent (all of which sell contacts to publishers).
- **Pricing:** paid subscription, **no free tier and no freemium.** We deliberately do **not** match
  SponsorGap's free search; our top-of-funnel is a no-signup sample lookup (teaser) + early access, then
  paid (Pro ~$99/mo annual, Team ~$299/mo, Agency custom) with a 14-day money-back guarantee. See `profile.md`.
